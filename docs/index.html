Introduction

Harry Potter is the most selling book series of all time. With more than 500 million copies sold the wizard Harry Potter is beloved by many. In the video below we will give a small introduction to the work done in this project.

The school of wizardry, Hogwarts, is divided into four different houses. Slytherin, Ravenclaw, Griffendor and Hufflepuff, each with its own charateristics. We will in this project investigate, using network theory and sentiment analysis, the differences between these houses.

We gathered the data from the Harry Potter Fandom wikia by scraping all the characters within the category Individuals by Hogwarts house , while keeping track of which house each character came from. This gives as all the characters from the houses Griffindor, Hufflepuff, Ravenclaw, Slytherin, and all the characters whose house we don't know.

Graph

By converting each character's Wikipedia page into a node, and the hyperlinks between those nodes as edges, we are able to create the graph.

In the interactive graph below the characters are colored according to their official house color, and you can zoom in an out of the graph, and highlight a node to see which charater it is.

The graph above contains 851 nodes and 4495 edges, but as it can be seen from the graph there is a large amount of nodes with degree of 0, that is floating around the rest of the graph like an asteroid field. If we disregard the nodes with a degree of 0, we end up with 521 nodes.

Basic Graph Statistics

In this section we will delve into some basic graph statistics, to provide more inside of the social network of Hogwarts.

In the graph above we see the in and out degree distributions for each node of the respective houses. As its seen from the graphs most of the nodes have an in-degree and out-degree in the first bin. This is due the fact that, as mentioned earlier, only 521 of the total 851 nodes have a degree of more than zero, so all 330 nodes without any edges will end up in the first bin. From the graphs it seems that only Griffindor and Slytherin have characters with a high in or out degree, but only a few characters have a out-degree of 20 or above, and only a few characters have a in-degree of 30 or above.

In the figure above we have the out-degree as a function of the in-degree. Generally the in and out degree seems to be nicely correlated, but an interesting thing to note is the fact that Griffindor generally have a higher out-degree compared to their in-degree. The Griffindor characters consistently link to other characters more than the other houses, this could be an indicator that the Griffindor characters generally interact more with other characters than the characters from the other houses.

In the figures above we have the in and out-degree on non-log and log scale. The degree distributation of a random network model is well described by a poisson distribution. $$p _ { k } = e ^ { - \langle k \rangle } \frac { \langle k \rangle ^ { k } } { k ! }$$ But the random network model, underestimates the frequency of high degree nodes of real world networks. The degree distribution of real world networks are often follow a power law distribution, defined as $$p _ { k } \sim k ^ { - \gamma }$$ The power law distribution is a straight downward trending line when plotted on a log-log scale. When looking at our distribution in the plot above, it's clear that the network doesn't follow the poission distribution, as we have too many nodes with a very high degree. Our network is more closely following a power law, in the way the there is linear downwarding trend on the log-log scale.

In table above we can inspect the betweeness centrality, which is a measure of how many nodes need to pass through that node in order to get to another node. The table also displays the eigenvector centrality, which is a measure of how many "important" nodes link to that node.

As it can be seen in the table Harry Potter is number 1 in all 4 columns, which makes sense given his very central role in the movies. Tom Riddle (Lord Voldemort) has the second highest eigenvector centrality, while only having the 11th highest betweeness centrality, this is interresting in the way that a lot of high-degree nodes link to him, but he hasn't got a very high betweeness centrality.

Community Detection

To investigate the communities that exist within the social network of Hogwarts we will use the Louvain algorithm to partition the network into various clusters. To simplify the network we remove all of the nodes with a degree of zero, as these nodes would all be assigned to their own community.

By applying the Louvain community detection we divide the graph into 31 seperate communities, and achieve a modularity of 0.318, which tells us that these communities are not very well defined.

In the graph above we can inspect which nodes belong to which communities. By looking at the graph we can see that many of students are being assigned their own community. Ronald Weasley, Hermione Granger, Neville Longbottom and Ginevra Weasley are all students, and they a being assigned to their own community.

The teachers of Hogwarts are also being assigned to the same community. Examples of these teachers are Severus Snape, Albus Dumbledore, Rubus Hagrid and Minerva Mcgonagall.

Generally it seems that the community detection algorithm is able to cluster the characters that interact with each other in the movie. The communities don't capture which houses the characters belong to, but this might be due to the fact that the louvain community detection algorithm only assigns each node to a single community. In reality the characters are all members of many smaller communities, so investigating other multi-community-detection algorithms might be interresting as a future work.

Sentiment Analysis

Sentiment analysis can be used to inspect the sentiment of a given text. Many words have a certain sentiment, such as the word "love", which has a very high sentiment score, and the the word "hate" which has a very low sentiment score. The word list LabMT generated by Amazon's Mechanical Turk, contains the 5000 most frequent words in a number of sources, and Mechanical Turk was used to generate a sentiment score for each word. We can use this list to calculate the average sentiment of a given text, by looking up the sentiment of every word and taking the average.

In the figure above we see the average sentiment of each of 4 houses, and the character whos house we don't know. As seen from the bar plot, all of the houses have almost the same sentiment. This is probably due to the fact that all Wikipedia articles are written in a very informative way, and thus the sentiment should be objective/neutral. There are however a few differences, as seen in the figure, Hufflepuffs have the highest sentiment, characters that we don't know the house of, have the lowest sentiment.

We can inspect the sentiment a little closer, by looking at the distribution of the sentiment across characters as opposed to just the average.

From the figure above we see that Gryffindor and Slytherin generally have approximately the same sentiment scores, but Slytherin have some additional negative outliers. In fact slytherin is generally more spread out, in the way that the loweset and highest sentiment character are both from the Slytherin house.

We will now inspect the sentiment of each of the characters. As discussed earlier, the sentiment is an average of each of the words, meaning that characters with short Wikipedia pages have a higher chance of getting either the best or the worst sentiment score, and as these characters are not important to the movies, we will only look at the nodes that have a degree of more than 10.

In the table above we have the 10 characters with a degree higher than 10, that have the highest sentiment. As it can be seen from the table most of these characters are from Slytherin, which is pretty surprising, considering the fact that Slytherin is usually considered the evil house, but it turns out that they actually also have a lot of high sentiment characters.

In the table above we have the 10 characters with a degree higher than 10, that have the lowest sentiment. Once again we see that that slytherin dominate the outliers. Almost the entire top 10 of lowest sentiment characters are from Slytherin. This confirms what we observed in the distribution plot, that slytherin are characters' sentiment, are more spread out, meaning that both the nicest and the evilest characters are from slytherin.

Word Inspection

In this section we will investigate which words define each of the houses. A simple way of defining words that define a house, could be to just count the most common words. We could define this as $$ \mathrm { tf } ( t , d ) = f _ { t , d } $$ where \(f _ { t , d }\) is the frequency of term, \(t\), in document \(d\). The downside of this approach is that these words don't uniquely identify a house, as all the houses would probably have almost the same words. A way to solve this problem is to use TF-IDF, which stands for term-frequency inverse-document-frequency, he we introduce the term $$ \operatorname { idf } ( t , D ) = \log \frac { N } { | \{ d \in D : t \in d \} | } $$ where \(N\) is the number of documents in the corpus, and \(| \{ d \in D : t \in d \} |\) is the number of documents where term \(t\) appears.

We can now obtain the complete tf-idf by taking the product $$ \operatorname { tfidf } ( t , d , D ) = \operatorname { tf } ( t , d ) \cdot \operatorname { idf } ( t , D ) $$ We can use this tf-idf score to generate word-clouds for each house, with the most unique words for each house.

Transcripts

We wanted to see if we could find a correlation between the language in the films. It could be very interesting to see if the films becomes darker and more terrifing as the stories continues, or make connections between the movie and the sentiment analysis. We do this by using Wikia transcript to download the Harry Potter scripts.

You can click on the movies below and inspect the sentiment graph. We calculate the sentiment every 4 lines, so the number on the x-axis is the line number times four.

If we try to compare the first movie (Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone) with the last movie (Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows: Part 2) it is easy to see a big difference in the language. In the first movie, the general sentiment is around 5.5 during the whole movie. There is no real big outliers anywhere. The last movies sentiment is very noisy and it obvious to see that the language is very different. An explanation for this could be that the first movie is more of a childrens movie, as the actors are younger and therefore the language is more neutral. The newest movies is more a teenage/adult movie, where romance, drama and action is very dominant. This makes the language a lot more agressive and this what we might be seeing in the graphs.

We can also take a look at only the last movie. Here there are some very obvious negative spikes around 50 and 300. The first spike is when they are inside the goblin vault, and Hermione comes up with the plan to fly away on the dragon. This is a turning point and therefore positive words comes up. The second spike is when Harry Potter dies. This is very sad moment in the movie and that is also reflected by the language. It is interesting to try and relate these spikes to what is acutally happening in the movies.

Generally all the movies goes up and down at a steady rythm but some are more exaggerate.
